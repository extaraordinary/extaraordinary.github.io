About

My research explores how disorder, complexity, and atomic-scale structure govern compositional stability, vibrational behavior, and thermal transport in materials. I am broadly interested in developing predictive frameworks for phonons in chemically complex systems, where conventional assumptions of periodicity and simplicity no longer hold. By integrating first-principles calculations, high-performance computing, and machine-learning approaches, I seek to elucidate how vibrational phenomena emerge in disordered materials and how they can be deliberately engineered for energy applications, with the long-term goal of contributing to a more sustainable and equitable future.

My doctoral work centers on the predictive modeling of high-entropy oxides for thermal energy conversion, with a particular emphasis on phonons and vibrational spectra. I employ ab initio methods in conjunction with scalable, high-throughput workflows to probe vibrational properties across expansive compositional spaces. Increasingly, my research incorporates density-functional theory alongside machine learning interatomic potentials (MLIPs)—particularly graph neural networks—to accelerate phonon calculations and enable the study of systems that would otherwise be computationally prohibitive.

A recurring theme in my research is the tradeoff between physical interpretability and computational efficiency. Within this context, I am particularly motivated by tackling fundamental questions such as: How can vibrational modes be meaningfully defined in strongly disordered materials? What information is lost when complexity is averaged out, and what new descriptors are required to capture emergent thermal behavior? Likewise, how can AI models be designed to respect physical constraints while fully leveraging the richness of large, heterogeneous datasets?

Notwithstanding the challenging nature, I regard these not as obstacles, but as opportunities to rethink how we model materials at the atomistic level. Alongside these already way too broad questions, my work also addresses more targeted methodological approaches to understanding phonons in disordered systems, including phonon band unfolding, non-analytical corrections to dynamical matrices, and the virtual crystal approximation. My research is inherently collaborative and interdisciplinary, and I actively engage with both experimentalists and theorists through the Center for Nanoscale Science, a National Science Foundation—supported Materials Research Science and Engineering Center (MRSEC), as well as other multi-institutional research efforts.

I am always happy to discuss research, cool ideas, or potential collaborations, and I welcome any conversations that challenge me. Feel free to reach out to me! :)

Fun

I love playing the piano and exploring a wide range of music from the romantic masterpieces of Frédéric Chopin and Franz Liszt to the evocative contemporary works of Hans Zimmer, Ludovico Einaudi and Yann Tiersen. Occasionally, I enjoy composing minimalistic music pieces of my own, or covering modern songs that resonate with me.

Traveling and learning about cultures is a source of constant inspiration for me. I enjoy wandering, uncovering local art, savoring regional cuisine, and find equal delight in absorbing the rhythms of daily life through photography. I also enjoy hiking, camping and skiing, in whatever way I can connect with nature.

I have recently discovered a deep appreciation for woodworking, and find it both grounding and rewarding. Learning about CMU's TechSpark makerspace was a natural entry point, and I had the opportunity of being offered the tools and community to turn ideas into some cool tangible objects. Some of my recent projects include a plant stand, a minimalist wooden crate, and a functional folding stool (folded view).

Reading remains a cherished refuge of mine. I am drawn to fiction novels and philosophy, which together enrich my understanding of the world and the human experience. I am also on goodreads!

About

My research explores how “disorder”, complexity, and atomic-scale structure govern compositional stability and heat transport in materials. I am broadly interested in developing predictive frameworks for phonons in chemically complex systems, where conventional assumptions of periodicity and simplicity no longer hold. By integrating first-principles calculations, high-performance computing, and machine learning approaches, I seek to elucidate how vibrational phenomena emerge in disordered materials and how they can be deliberately engineered for energy applications, with the long-term goal of contributing to a more sustainable and equitable future.

My doctoral work centers on the predictive modeling of high-entropy oxides for thermal energy conversion, with a particular emphasis on phonons. Ab initio methods—particularly, density functional theory calculations—are employed with scalable, high-throughput workflows to probe vibrational properties across expansive compositional spaces. Additionally, graph-neural-network-based machine learning interatomic potentials are incorporated to accelerate calculations and facilitate the study of systems that would otherwise be computationally prohibitive.

A recurring theme in my research is the tradeoff between physical interpretability and computational efficiency. Within this context, I am particularly motivated by tackling fundamental questions such as: How can one meaningfully define vibrational modes in strongly disordered materials? What information is lost when complexity is described by the parent components, and what new descriptors are required to capture emergent behavior? Likewise, how can ML models be designed to respect physical constraints while leveraging large datasets?

Notwithstanding the challenging nature, I regard these not as obstacles, but as opportunities to rethink how we model materials at the atomistic level. Alongside these already-way-too-broad questions, my work also addresses more targeted methodological approaches to understanding phonons in disordered systems, including phonon band unfolding, non-analytical corrections to the dynamical matrices, and the virtual crystal approximation. My research is inherently collaborative and interdisciplinary, and I actively engage with both experimentalists and theorists within the Center for Nanoscale Science, a National Science Foundation—supported Materials Research Science and Engineering Center (MRSEC), as well as other multi-institutional research efforts.

I am always happy to discuss research, cool ideas, or potential collaborations, and I welcome any conversations that challenge me. Definitely reach out to me! :-)

Fun

Playing the piano has long been a central part of my life. I enjoy exploring a wide range of music that spans from the Romantic era—particularly the works of Chopin and Liszt—to contemporary composers such as Hans Zimmer, Max Richter, Ludovico Einaudi, and Yann Tiersen. Occasionally, I compose minimalist pieces of my own or reinterpret modern songs that resonate with me. You can listen to a performance of Claude Debussy's Piano Trio in G Major by our trio chamber ensemble, as well as our piano quintet's interpretation of Queen's Bohemian Rhapsody.

Traveling and learning about new cultures constantly inspires me. I find delight in wandering around new places, discovering local art, savoring regional cuisine, and capturing the quiet rythms of daily life through photography. I also enjoy hiking, camping, and skiing; any excuse to be outdoors and feel connected to nature.

I have recently discovered a deep appreciation for woodworking, and find it truly grounding and rewarding. Learning about CMU's TechSpark makerspace was a natural entry point, and I had the opportunity of benefiting from the tools and community to turn ideas into some cool, tangible objects. Some of my recent projects are a plant stand, a minimalist wooden crate, and a functional folding stool (folded view).

Reading remains a cherished refuge of mine. I am drawn to fiction novels and philosophy, which together enrich my understanding of the world and the human experience. I cannot begin to describe how much reading has shaped who I am and how I view things since years ago. I am also on goodreads!

Feel free to take a look at my short article about learning and intellectual humility on Medium.
